The O is the object that roars.

The name gives us three Os to play with — the goal scream, "root for," the Merkle root, the roots. This lab treats the middle Os of R‑OOO‑T as the mark itself. Retro grammar (Topps · Panini · Mexico 70), modern execution (real geometry, perfect curves, no fake aging). Monochrome + gold; team colors only as accents. Eight directions built, then a kill round down to four. 16px legibility is a kill criterion — tested honestly on the tab strips below.

The two studies steering everything: the Mexico 70 ticket ball — a ball abstracted into a flat black pinwheel on a white disc, pure pop geometry, no leather realism — and the 1970 pennant, where that same geometric ball sits inside the 0 of "1970." Proof the ball-as-O already existed in the reference world. We're modernising a move the era invented.

◆The kill round — why these four survived

Survivors: A · Geo‑Ball, B · Stadium‑Bowl, C · Roar‑O, D · Knit‑O. Each clears the 16px gate with a purpose‑built favicon, each is a single squarable glyph, each works as the middle Os of R‑OOO‑T, and each covers a different axis of the brief: A owns soccer‑ness (it's the ball), B owns the‑roaring‑thing (it's the stadium), C owns sound + continuity (it's the incumbent, honest), D owns tiny‑size + material (it's the relic).

The four cut: The Mouth (kitsch risk + 16px blob), The Chant (a lockup, not a glyph), Kickoff Mark (too generic to own; promoted to the stage), Goal‑Mouth (detail dies below 40px). Their good ideas were re‑homed as motion or stage ceremony rather than thrown away.

My pick: B · Stadium‑Bowl. It's the only candidate where the mark, the concept, and the product are one object: the stadium is the O, the O is the thing that roars, and the pitch we're already rendering nests inside it — the halfway line is the 50/50 the whole app is about. Retro grammar (flat aerial geometry, à la the Mexico‑70 pictograms), modern execution.

Who the owner likely picks: C · Roar‑O — they said the concentric rings are liked as data language, and this keeps that equity while answering "make it more soccer/sound." It's the lowest‑risk evolution of what exists. If they want the bigger swing, steer them to B; if they want the ball to be unmistakable, A. My honest read: the winner is probably a Bowl exterior with a Roar‑O soul — a stadium ring that radiates when it roars. Flagged for the coordinator as the merge to prototype next.

The data‑vs‑design rule (REFERENCES.md · rule 8)

A real ticket is a designed base with your data impact‑printed onto it. Same split everywhere in ROOOT: the design speaks in the display faces (Anybody, Young Serif); the fan's data prints in the dot voice (Doto on screen & print, Silkscreen when knitted). Semantically honest — the machine prints what happened; the design holds it. Serials, seats, percentages, minutes are always dot‑printed; never set a probability in a display face, never dress a display face as data.

Do / Don't

Do

  • Retro grammar, modern execution — real geometry, perfect curves, honest print grain
  • Flat pop shapes (Topps / Panini / Mexico‑70 pictogram)
  • Monochrome + gold; one team‑accent pair per moment
  • Flags as blocks & stripes; unicode flags fine
  • Keep the mark legible at favicon size — test at 16px first
  • Grain everywhere, subtly (the rain/sweat register)

Don't

  • No crests, club marks, FIFA marks, rings, or player likenesses
  • No gradients inside the mark (flat fills only)
  • No mascot / emoji kitsch — no teeth, no faces
  • No fake aging, sepia, or distressed "vintage" filters
  • No betting‑app / dashboard / AI‑gradient look
  • Never blend crowd counts with market probability

React terse: "B, but simpler interior" · "merge B+C, prototype it" · "A — the ball has to win" · "Knit‑O everywhere". Whatever survives becomes design/IDENTITY.md and every lane builds the mark in its material — light on the stage, stitches on the scarf, ink on print.
